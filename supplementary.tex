\section{Hyperparameters Tuning}
\label{supp-section:hyperparameter-tuning}

All hyper parameter tunings were performed on a GTX3090 with 250 epochs, batch size of 16, with ADAM optimizer with learning rate of 0.0001. 

\subsection{Positional Encoding Methods}

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
%\resizebox{\columnwidth}{!}{%
\begin{tabular}{cccc}
\hline
            & \textbf{MSE}    & \textbf{SSIM}   & \textbf{PCC}    \\ \hline
Constant    & 0.0048          & 0.6504          & 0.9645          \\
Monotonic   & 0.0039          & 0.6732          & 0.9705          \\
Transformer & \textbf{0.0037} & 0.6699          & \textbf{0.9725} \\
Graph       & 0.0040          & \textbf{0.6746} & 0.9717          \\ \hline
\end{tabular}
%}
\caption{
    \label{supp-tab:positional-encodings} We retrained our model with four different positional encoding schemes. Constant positional encoding scheme assigns the same positional embeddings to all nodes where as monotonic positional encoding scheme assigns monontonically increasing positional encodings. Transformer positional encoding assumes the nodes to be in a linear sequence and uses sine and cosine functions to construct a unique vector for each node location. Lastly, we used graph positional encodings, which assigns a eigen vector of the input graph to each node. In our evaluations we show that the positional encoding scheme developed specifically for graphs provides the best improvement in the SSIM score. 
}
\end{table}

\subsection{Effects Node feature merging methods}
\begin{table}[H]
\centering
%\resizebox{\columnwidth}{!}{%
\begin{tabular}{cccc}
\hline
                                           & \textbf{MSE}    & \textbf{SSIM}   & \textbf{PCC}    \\ \hline
\textbf{None}                              & 0.0059          & 0.6213          & 0.9541          \\
\textbf{Positional Only}                   & 0.0045          & 0.6622          & 0.9676          \\
\textbf{Epigenetic Only}                   & 0.0049          & 0.6502          & 0.9644          \\
\textbf{Positional + Epigenetic (Sum)}     & 0.0042          & 0.6687          & 0.9697          \\ 
\textbf{Positional + Epigenetic (Concat)}  & \textbf{0.0040} & \textbf{0.6746} & \textbf{0.9717} \\
\textbf{Positional + Epigenetic (Average)} & 0.0042          & 0.6686          & 0.9694         \\\hline 
\end{tabular}
%}
\caption{
    \label{supp-tab:encoding-merging-methods} In this experiment we empirically evaluated the value of  having positional encodings, epigenetic encodings and what are some of the interesting ways we can merge them against the absolute baseline of not having any node encodings. We found that having some form of node identity (either positional or epigenetic) helped GrapHiC to do better predictions. Interestingly we found concatenating rather than summing, which is suggested in NLP literature, to be the most helpful for our model to do better predictions. 
}
\end{table}

\subsection{Choice of Graph Convolution Layer}
\begin{table}[H]
\centering
%\resizebox{\columnwidth}{!}{%

\hline
                         & \textbf{MSE}    & \textbf{SSIM}   & \textbf{PCC}    \\ \hline

\textbf{GraphConv}       & 0.0042          & 0.6655          & 0.9697          \\

\textbf{TransformerConv} & \textbf{0.0039} & \textbf{0.6770} & \textbf{0.9719} \\ \hline
\end{tabular}
%}
\caption{
    \label{supp-tab:conv-operator-eval} In this experiment we explored a set of readily available Graph Convolution operators to find convolution operator that works the best for our scenario. Emperically, we found that TransformerConv gave us the best performance among the four we tested. We believe that was the case because TransformerConv has learnable parameters for self node encoding, neighboring node encodings and also the edge features. Having discrete learnable parameters is absent in the other three we tested. 
}
\end{table}

\begin{table}[H]

\hline
            & \textbf{MSE}    & \textbf{SSIM}   & \textbf{PCC}    \\ \hline
\textbf{4}  & 0.0128          & 0.5247          & 0.8879          \\
\textbf{8}  & 0.0063          & 0.6160          & 0.9518          \\
\textbf{16} & 0.0047          & 0.6464          & 0.9654          \\
\textbf{32} & \textbf{0.0039} & \textbf{0.6770} & \textbf{0.9719} \\
\textbf{64} & CUDA\_OOM       & CUDA\_OOM       & CUDA\_OOM       \\ \hline
\end{tabular}
%}
\caption{
    \label{supp-tab:embedding-size-eval} In this experiment we evaluated how our model performed with embeddings of various sizes. These embedding size represent the size of node encodings across all the encoder layer and the number of filters in all the decoder residual blocks. We see a linear increase in the model performance as we increase the size of the embeddings but unfortunately we run out of GPU memory when we try to run our model with embeddings of size 64. Consequently, given all of our results in previous sections, we free our model architecture at 3 encoder transformer blocks followed by 5 decoder residual blocks all of them with embeddings of size 32.  
}
\end{table}
